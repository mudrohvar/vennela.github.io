My Product Management Philosophy

These principles guide how I approach product management, make decisions, and build teams. Developed over years at Google, Amazon, EY, and Robustwealth, they represent what I've learned about building products that create lasting value.

Core Beliefs

Impact Over Output

Shipping features doesn't equal creating value. I measure success by the problems solved and the impact created, not by the number of features shipped or story points completed.

In practice, this means I'd rather ship one well-researched, beautifully executed feature that delights users and moves business metrics than ten half-baked features that clutter the product. At Google, we cut our roadmap from 40 features to 8‚Äîand paradoxically shipped faster and had more impact.

"Great PMs understand that what you choose NOT to build is just as important as what you build."

User Empathy is Non-Negotiable

You can't build great products for users you don't understand. Data tells you what is happening; talking to users tells you why it's happening.

I make it a point to talk to at least 5 users every week, regardless of how busy I am. These conversations have saved me from building the wrong thing more times than I can count. At Amazon, a single user interview revealed that our proposed checkout "improvement" would actually frustrate power users‚Äîsaving us from a costly mistake.

My approach: Treat every user conversation as an opportunity to uncover insights, not validate assumptions. Ask "why" five times. Listen more than you talk. Observe behavior, not just what people say they do.
üìä

Data-Informed, Not Data-Driven

Data is a powerful tool, but context matters more than numbers. Being data-driven without understanding the "why" behind the metrics can lead to optimizing for the wrong things.

At Amazon, I learned to love metrics. But I also learned that metrics can lie. A 15% improvement in click-through rate sounds great‚Äîuntil you realize it came from a dark pattern that eroded long-term trust. I use data to inform decisions, but I combine quantitative insights with qualitative understanding, strategic thinking, and sometimes gut instinct earned through experience.

Key questions I always ask: What's the metric measuring? What's it not capturing? What incentives does this metric create? How could this be gamed? What's the qualitative feedback saying?
üöÄ

Ship Fast, Learn Faster

Perfect is the enemy of shipped. The best way to learn is to get your product in front of real users, not to endlessly debate in conference rooms.

I'm a strong believer in iterative development. Ship the smallest version that delivers value, gather feedback, and iterate. This doesn't mean shipping broken products‚Äîit means being thoughtful about what "good enough" looks like for a first version. At Robustwealth, we launched our mobile app with 40% of the planned features but got it into users' hands 3 months earlier. The feedback we gathered shaped a much better product than we would have built in isolation.

"The market doesn't care about your roadmap. Ship, learn, adjust. Repeat."

Product is a Team Sport

The best products emerge from collaboration, not hero PMs. Engineering, design, data science, marketing‚Äî everyone brings unique perspectives that make the product better.

I see my role as enabling the team, not dictating to them. Engineers often have the best product ideas because they understand technical possibilities. Designers see user friction that I miss. Data scientists uncover patterns that inform strategy. My job is to create alignment, provide context, and empower everyone to contribute their best thinking.

How I build trust: Give credit generously. Admit when I'm wrong. Seek input before making decisions. Protect the team from distractions. Celebrate wins together, take ownership of failures myself.

My Decision-Making Framework

How I approach product decisions, from feature prioritization to strategic bets

1

Understand the Problem Deeply

Before jumping to solutions, I spend time understanding the root problem. What's the user trying to accomplish? Why is the current solution failing? What constraints exist? I use the "5 Whys" technique and talk to multiple stakeholders to ensure I'm solving the real problem, not just symptoms.

2

Gather Diverse Perspectives

I actively seek out disagreement. Engineers might see technical solutions I haven't considered. Designers might propose UX approaches that reframe the problem. Support teams hear user pain points daily. I create space for all voices and actively listen to perspectives different from my own.

3

Evaluate Against Strategic Goals

Every decision should ladder up to company strategy. I ask: Does this move us closer to our North Star? How does this impact our key metrics? What's the opportunity cost? I use frameworks like RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) to make prioritization objective and defensible.

4

Make the Decision & Communicate Clearly

After gathering input, I make a decision and clearly communicate the "why" behind it. I document the reasoning, the alternatives considered, and the criteria used. This creates alignment and helps the team understand how to make similar decisions independently in the future.

5

Ship, Measure, Learn, Iterate

I'm comfortable being wrong if we learn quickly. I set clear success criteria upfront, measure rigorously after launch, and am willing to change course based on data. Some of my best product decisions came from admitting a previous decision was wrong and pivoting based on what we learned.

These Principles in Action

Real examples of how these beliefs shaped product decisions

Google: Cutting the Roadmap by 80%

When I joined the GCP team, we had 40 features on the roadmap for the next quarter. After talking to users and analyzing usage data, I realized 32 of those features were "nice to haves" that wouldn't move the needle on our core metrics.

I proposed cutting the roadmap to 8 features that would genuinely improve developer experience. It was a hard sell‚Äîstakeholders had pet projects on the list. But by focusing on impact over output, we shipped those 8 features with exceptional quality, increased user satisfaction by 30 points, and ironically shipped faster because we weren't context-switching constantly.

Amazon: Killing a Feature After Launch

We shipped a new checkout feature that showed a 12% increase in one-click purchases. The data looked great. But qualitative feedback told a different story‚Äîusers felt manipulated, and trust scores were declining.

I made the call to kill the feature despite the positive metrics. Short-term wins aren't worth long-term trust erosion. We redesigned the feature to be more transparent, and while the conversion lift was only 6%, user sentiment improved significantly. This reinforced my belief in being data-informed, not data-driven.

Robustwealth: Shipping the MVP in 3 Months

The initial plan for our mobile app was a 9-month timeline with 60+ features. I pushed back hard, arguing that we could ship a valuable product in 3 months with just 25 core features.

We launched the lean version, and the feedback was invaluable. Users wanted features we hadn't even considered, and several "must-have" features on our original roadmap went completely unused. By shipping fast and learning, we built a better product than we would have by following the original plan.

Principles Evolve With Experience

These principles aren't set in stone. They've evolved as I've learned from successes, failures, and countless conversations with users, teammates, and other PMs. I'm sure they'll continue to evolve as I take on new challenges and learn new things.

What won't change is my commitment to putting users first, building with integrity, and creating products that genuinely make people's lives better.
